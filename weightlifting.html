Weightlifting Fundamentals

Learn the basic principles of proper weightlifting technique and form to maximize gains and prevent injuries.

Weighlifting comes in many shapes an sizes, but on this page the focus will be strength training vs hypertrophy training. Both strength and hypertrophy training have their own reasons for use.
Strength Training: lower reps, higher weights, focus on moving larger amounts of weight
Strength training uses higher weights to force your muscles to work harder, not only training your muscles for greater weight, but also your mind to be able to handle more weight.
HyperTrophy Training: Higher reps, slightly lower weights, focusing on squeezing and stretching the muscle
Hypertrophy training on the other hand focuses on the squeeze. With a better contraction, pauses in your movement, or greater stretches in movements, your msucles can grow more in size, but may lack some strength to keep form perfect.

Strength and Hypertrophy have their own methods to training

Complete Weightlifting Guide

Training Frequency

Beginners: 3 full-body sessions/week
Intermediate: 4-5 days (split routine)
Advanced: 5-6 days (specialized splits)

Essential Exercises

  • Barbell Back Squats
  • Romanian Deadlifts
  • Bench Press
  • Overhead Press
  • Pull-ups/Pulldowns
  • Rows (Barbell/Dumbbell)

Progression Model

Strength: 2-4 sets of 3-5 reps
Hypertrophy: 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps
Endurance: 2-3 sets of 15-20 reps

Beginner Full-Body Routine

Workout A

  • Squat: 3x5
  • Bench Press: 3x5
  • Bent-over Row: 3x8

Workout B

  • Deadlift: 3x5
  • Overhead Press: 3x5
  • Pull-ups: 3xAMRAP

Alternate between A and B, 3x/week with at least one rest day between sessions

A balanced weekly weightlifting routine. Pick 3 from each column for a balanced day.

Form Factor

Learn why form is so important in weightlifting.

When lifting, how you hold your form is very important to not only completing the exercise but also keeping yourself safe. When you go off the "path" or form of and exercise, The weight can effect other muscles and pull them out of place, leaving you at a higher risk for injury
Concentric: When you are pushing or pulling the weight. Doing the hard work
The concentric of the exercise, as mentioned, is the pushing or pulling of the weight. Imagine a bench press, where the weight is already on your chest.
To get that weight off, you must contract your chest muscles, bringing your arms closer together, that the bar is pressed up. The concentric is where most strength gains are made
Eccentric: Gravity is moving the weight, you must control it
Now imagine that same bench press, but you are holding the weight up now, at the top of the rep. When gravity is bringing that weight down, you must slow it and control it on its way.
If you go to fast, it will take extra strength to stop that speed at the bottom of the rep, so controlling the eccentric is key. The eccentric is where youre muscles are stretching, and
a large part of how muscle hypertrophy is gained.
